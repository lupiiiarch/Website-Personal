Reported Speech

What is Reported Speech?

Reported speech (also called indirect speech) is used to report what someone else has said without quoting them directly. It is the opposite of direct speech.

  • Direct speech: He said, "I am tired."
  • Reported speech: He said that he was tired.

Tense Changes in Reported Speech

When we report what someone said, we usually shift the tense back (backshifting), especially when the reporting verb is in the past tense.

Direct Speech Reported Speech
Present Simple: "I work hard." He said that he worked hard.
Present Continuous: "I am cooking." She said she was cooking.
Past Simple: "I visited Paris." He said he had visited Paris.
Will: "I will help you." She said she would help me.

Pronoun Changes

Pronouns also need to change to match the speaker and context:

  • Direct: She said, "I love my job."
  • Reported: She said that she loved her job.

Common Reporting Verbs

  • Say: She said (that) she was happy.
  • Tell (needs an object): She told me (that) she was happy.
  • Ask: He asked me where I lived.
  • Suggest, advise, warn: often followed by a verb in base or -ing form.

Examples of Direct and Reported Speech

  • Direct: John said, "I’m learning English."
  • Reported: John said that he was learning English.
  • Direct: Mary asked, "Do you like pizza?"
  • Reported: Mary asked if I liked pizza.

Tips for Using Reported Speech

  • Check the reporting verb tense to decide if you need to backshift the verb tense.
  • Change pronouns and time expressions appropriately: "today" becomes "that day", "tomorrow" becomes "the next day".
  • Use "that" to introduce statements, and "if/whether" to introduce yes/no questions.
  • Don’t change the tense if the reporting verb is in the present or if the situation is still true.
