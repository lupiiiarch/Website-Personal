Conditional Sentences

Definition of Conditional Sentences

Conditional sentences are sentences that express a condition and the result that will occur if the condition is fulfilled. These sentences usually consist of two main parts:

  • If-clause (condition): The part that states the requirement or condition.
  • Main clause (result): The part that expresses the result or consequence of the condition.

The Four Main Types of Conditional Sentences

Conditional sentences are divided into four main types based on time and the likelihood of the condition happening:

  1. Zero Conditional: General facts and scientific truths.
  2. First Conditional: Real possibilities in the future.
  3. Second Conditional: Hypothetical situations in the present or future.
  4. Third Conditional: Hypothetical situations in the past (unreal).

Structure and Examples of Each Type

Type Structure Example Sentence Usage
Zero Conditional If + present simple, present simple If you heat water, it boils. Used to show facts or general truths.
First Conditional If + present simple, will + verb (infinitive) If it rains, I will stay home. Used for real future possibilities.
Second Conditional If + past simple, would + verb (infinitive) If I won the lottery, I would travel the world. Used for hypothetical situations in the present or future.
Third Conditional If + past perfect, would have + past participle If you had studied, you would have passed the exam. Used for unreal situations in the past.

Examples: Positive, Negative, and Questions

Zero Conditional

  • Positive: If you mix red and blue, you get purple.
  • Negative: If you don't water plants, they die.
  • Question: What happens if you heat ice?

First Conditional

  • Positive: If she calls, I will answer.
  • Negative: If it doesn't snow, we won't go skiing.
  • Question: Will you come if I invite you?

Second Conditional

  • Positive: If I were rich, I would buy a house.
  • Negative: If he didn't have a car, he wouldn't drive to work.
  • Question: Would you travel if you had more money?

Third Conditional

  • Positive: If they had left earlier, they would have caught the train.
  • Negative: If she hadn't eaten so much, she wouldn't have felt sick.
  • Question: Would you have come if I had invited you?
