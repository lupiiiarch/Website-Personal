Relative Pronouns

What Are Relative Pronouns?

Relative pronouns are words used to connect a clause or phrase to a noun or pronoun. They introduce relative clauses, which give more information about the noun.

List of Common Relative Pronouns

  • Who: Refers to people as the subject of the relative clause.
  • Whom: Refers to people as the object of the relative clause.
  • Which: Refers to animals or things.
  • That: Can refer to people, animals, or things in restrictive clauses.
  • Whose: Shows possession; can refer to people, animals, or things.

How to Use Relative Pronouns in Sentences

Relative pronouns introduce relative clauses that modify a noun or pronoun. There are two types of relative clauses:

  • Restrictive clauses: Essential to the meaning of the sentence. Usually no commas are used.
  • Non-restrictive clauses: Provide extra information, usually separated by commas.

Example:

  • Restrictive: The book that you gave me is interesting.
  • Non-restrictive: My car, which is red, is parked outside.

Examples of Relative Clauses and Their Functions

  • Who: The woman who lives next door is a doctor. (Refers to a person, subject)
  • Whom: The man whom you met yesterday is my uncle. (Refers to a person, object)
  • Which: The movie which won the award was excellent. (Refers to things or animals)
  • That: The house that Jack built is old. (Restrictive clause for people/things)
  • Whose: The student whose phone rang apologized. (Shows possession)

Difference Between 'That' and 'Which'

That is used in restrictive clauses (essential information) without commas:

  • The book that has a blue cover is mine.

Which is used in non-restrictive clauses (extra information) with commas:

  • The book, which has a blue cover, is mine.

Note: In American English, this distinction is very important. In British English, sometimes which is used in restrictive clauses as well.
